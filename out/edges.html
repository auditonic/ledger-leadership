The Five Edges

Each edge cuts ambiguity in a specific direction. They are tools, not concepts.

These edges are not sequential. Any conversation can enter through any edge. Mastery is knowing which edge to apply when.

If it didn't occur in the world, it doesn't exist in the ledger. Each edge below forces that reckoning in a different dimension.

1

Reality Edge

"What actually happened?"

What this is NOT asking

Not why it happened Not who caused it Not what we hoped Not what we planned

This edge kills

  • Performative alignment — where everyone nods but no one agrees
  • Retrospective storytelling — rewriting history to match the narrative
  • Status-based truth — where the highest-paid person's version wins

How you know it's dull

Different people in the same meeting leave with different understandings of what was decided. Reports describe what should have happened, not what did.

Example moment

"Before we discuss solutions—what specifically occurred? Not interpretations. Events."

2

Obligation Edge

"Who carries the consequence when this fails?"

What this is NOT asking

Not ownership charts Not job titles Not who's "responsible" Not org chart lines

This edge exposes

  • Orphaned decisions — choices that belong to no one
  • Soft authority — people with titles but no real power
  • Responsibility diffusion — "everyone owns it" means no one does

How you know it's dull

When something fails, there's a scramble to figure out who should have caught it. Accountability conversations happen after the fact, not before.

Example moment

"If this doesn't work, whose problem does it become? Name the person, not the team."

3

Continuity Edge

"What must persist for this to keep working tomorrow?"

What this is NOT asking

Not success today Not heroics Not one-time effort Not "we'll figure it out"

This edge cuts through

  • Burnout cultures — where success depends on unsustainable effort
  • Fragile excellence — outcomes that collapse when one person leaves
  • "We'll figure it out later" thinking — deferred system design

How you know it's dull

The same crisis recurs every quarter. Success stories can't be repeated by different people. Vacation and sick days create emergencies.

Example moment

"If the person who built this left tomorrow, what breaks? What's not written down?"

4

Margin Edge

"How close are we to failure right now?"

What this is NOT asking

Not optimism Not confidence Not what we hope Not best-case scenario

This edge destroys

  • False resilience — "we've always made it work" without examining how close it was
  • Overcommitment — saying yes without understanding the buffer consumed
  • Silent risk stacking — small risks accumulating unnoticed

How you know it's dull

"Surprises" happen regularly. Small delays cascade into major problems. There's no slack in the system—everything runs at capacity.

Example moment

"What's our buffer if one thing goes wrong? Not two things—one. Do we have any margin?"

5

Reconciliation Edge

"What are we pretending balances that doesn't?"

What this is asking

The unspoken gaps The agreed-upon fictions The residue from decisions The debt from meetings

This edge forces

  • Naming the gap — saying out loud what everyone suspects
  • Cooling organizational fever — slowing down when motion has become a substitute for progress
  • Truth before motion — requiring clarity before action

How you know it's dull

Everyone knows there's a problem, but it's never on the agenda. Meetings end with vague next steps that no one follows up on. The same issues resurface in different language.

Example moment

"What's the thing we're not saying in this room? The gap everyone sees but no one names?"

When All Five Edges Are Sharp

Debate collapses. Politics starve. Clarity accelerates.

No one has to win. Reality already has.

How the Edges Relate

  • They're not sequential—any conversation can enter through any edge
  • One dull edge often masks another (unclear reality hides unclear obligation)
  • Sharpening one edge often reveals where others have dulled
  • In crisis, start with Reality. In planning, start with Continuity. In conflict, start with Reconciliation.
